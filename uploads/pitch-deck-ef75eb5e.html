Competitive landscape

Everything today assumes you still have your device

Medical ID / Emergency SOS

Brilliant, but it lives on your phone. When the phone is stolen, your emergency contacts go with it.

Find My / CEIR / IMEI blocking

Tracks and blocks the device. Recovery for later. Under 15% of phones in India are ever recovered. Useless in the moment.

Lock-screen contact apps (FINDERR, ICE)

Help a finder return your phone. A thief is not a finder.

The gap

No product lets a victim use a borrowed phone to reach their people, with zero memorization beyond their own number. This is the moment Emergency List owns.

Step zero · one-time setup

Your Emergency List:
set up once, when you set up your iPhone

  • Part of iPhone onboarding, right alongside Face ID and Apple ID
  • You register exactly three emergency contacts: the people who should get the call
  • Plus your number and a passcode you already remember
  • Face Check: a separate rescue face enrollment as your second factor, not your device Face ID
  • Optional: share your location (Find My) with any, all, or none of the three. Your choice, per contact
  • Stored in Apple's secure escrow, not on other people's phones

Important: a borrowed iPhone only ever surfaces these three names. Never your contact book, never the phone owner's contacts. Three registered people, nothing else.

Find My integration

Reach your people. Then find the phone

1 · Opt in at setupShare your location with any, all, or none of your three contacts. Per contact, your choice.
→
2 · They see itYour brother already sees your iPhone in Find My, even where you left it, even if it's offline.
→
3 · He pushes it to the caseOne tap: "Share Vasu's phone location to the caller." Only into an active, verified rescue session.
→
4 · You allow itThe borrowed iPhone asks: Allow or Dismiss. Allow, and the last known location opens as a pin in Maps.
→
5 · It disappearsCase closed: the shared location is erased with everything else.

Most "stolen" phones are just forgotten: the café table, the train seat. This turns "describe the place over the phone" into a pin on a map. And Apple already ships the pattern: iOS 18's Share Item Location creates exactly this kind of temporary, expiring location link.

9
Privacy by design

Built so a security review says yes

  • Nothing new to remember: your number + your passcode, both already in your head
  • Escrow-verified passcode: the same hardened-HSM pattern Apple ships today in iCloud Keychain recovery, with strict attempt limits
  • Face Check second factor: an opt-in rescue enrollment, separate from device Face ID, matched with liveness detection and used only during a help session
  • Numbers never displayed: names and relationships only, both directions
  • Human-in-the-loop: contacts approve before any call connects
  • Location only by double consent: opt-in per contact at setup, pushed only by the trusted contact, accepted on the borrowed iPhone, dead when the case closes
  • Ephemeral by architecture: the session exists only in memory on the borrowed iPhone; closing the case destroys everything, on both sides
  • No data business, ever: this data is never monetized, analyzed, or shared. Safety is the product.

Why Apple · why OS-level

Only Apple can build this, and it's worth building

Must ship with iOS

The whole point is that the stranger's phone already has it. No App Store install survives that requirement. This is a platform feature, like Emergency SOS.

The infrastructure exists

Secure escrow (iCloud Keychain), push verification, Face ID on the owner side for setup, satellite-era emergency UX. This is assembly, not invention.

Safety sells iPhones

Crash Detection and Satellite SOS are Apple's most powerful ads. "Lost your phone? Any iPhone can reach your family" is the next story in that lineage.

A network effect Android can't copy

Every iPhone makes every other iPhone owner safer. Feature value scales with iPhone density, strongest exactly where theft is worst (London, Indian metros, São Paulo).

In hotspot markets, theft anxiety is a point-of-sale objection. This feature converts it into a reason to buy.

The business case

Safety is the new reason people choose iPhone

A switching driver in exactly the right markets

In London, Indian metros, and São Paulo, theft anxiety is a live factor at the point of sale. "If my phone is stolen, any iPhone around me can reach my family" turns that fear into a reason to buy, and to stay. Feature exclusive to iOS: the more iPhones around you, the safer you are.

Android structurally can't answer

The feature's value depends on density: the stranger's phone must already have it. Apple ships one OS on 100% of its devices, with secure-escrow infrastructure in place. Android is split across dozens of manufacturers and OS versions; no vendor can promise "any nearby phone can help you."

Proven playbook

Crash Detection and Emergency SOS via Satellite became Apple's most effective advertising, with real "this saved my life" stories. iPhone retention already exceeds 90%; safety features deepen it and headline every keynote. This is the next story in that lineage.

Illustrative upside

The three hotspot regions hold hundreds of millions of smartphone buyers. If visible safety tips even just 1% of their annual purchases toward iPhone, that's on the order of a million incremental units, roughly $1 billion a year at current average selling prices, before any halo effect in other markets. (Illustrative estimate.)

Framing note: this is sold as consumer protection, not platform advantage: "you're safer on iPhone." The switching and retention follow the safety story on their own.
